What Happens When HSC Discovers a Hate Symbol

— Haven Duce

On the morning of 1 July 2026, we were walking along Barton Street with coffee when we came across a Star of David with "Lucifer" written below it and "Ishtar" above it—a clear antisemitic hate symbol designed to link Judaism with Satanism through centuries-old conspiracy theories. We flagged it immediately, called the Hamilton Police non-emergency line (905-546-4925) at 11:18 AM, and explained what we'd seen to dispatch.

Here's what we do when that happens. And why.

Our Policy: Hate Symbols Are Police Work, Not Business Work

We remove graffiti for our clients and the BIA. We remove tags, throws, pieces, stickers, accidental markers. We handle the wear-and-tear of storefronts. We carry spray paint for quick touch-ups, and we use it.

But we don't remove hate speech.

When we encounter a symbol designed to target, threaten, or dehumanize a person, a community, or a faith, that's not graffiti. That's a hate crime. It's evidence. It needs a legal response, a police report, and documentation through proper channels—not spray paint and a touch-up.

Our personal policy is clear: if we see hate speech, we don't touch it. We document it, we report it, and we step back.

What We Actually Do: The Process

Step 1: Document immediately. Take photos. Note the time, the exact location, what the symbols are, any surrounding context (nearby businesses, foot traffic, visibility from the street). Don't touch anything.

Step 2: Call Hamilton Police non-emergency. 905-546-4925. Explain what you've found. Be specific: describe the symbols, explain why you're flagging it as hate speech, give the location. You're not asking them to rush; you're reporting a crime.

Step 3: Explain your context. If you work for a BIA, manage a block, or have community ties to the area, say so. This helps dispatch understand the report isn't random—it's from someone who knows the neighborhood and its businesses.

Step 4: Wait. Police will take their time. They may not show up for hours. That's not a failure of the system; it's the system working. The graffiti is evidence. It needs to stay where it is until police can document and collect it.

Step 5: Cooperate fully. If police ask for a statement, give one. Provide your name, phone number, what you saw, when you saw it. If they take photos or ask follow-up questions, answer them. This is accountability.

Our Response — 1 July 2026

  • 11:18 AM: Discovered the symbol while walking Barton.
  • 11:30 AM: Called HPS non-emergency, spoke with dispatch, explained the symbol, our role with the BIA, and our policy.
  • 11:30–12:00 PM: Waited on site for roughly 30 minutes in case police responded quickly, then stepped back to let documentation happen.
  • 6:35 PM: Hamilton Police arrived on site and called us back. Officer requested an official statement.
  • 6:45 PM: During the statement, the officer asked exactly when we'd last been on that property and how frequently we work in the area. We were able to confirm we'd been on that exact location Monday afternoon (29 June 2026, 1430–1500). The symbol wasn't there then. We found it Wednesday morning at 11:18 AM. That means it was created sometime in a 43-hour window—Monday 1500 through Wednesday 1118. Not precise, but documented.
  • 7:00 PM: Police painted over the site.
  • Documentation: We recorded the police call number, the attending officer's name, and their badge number. Our regular monitoring schedule and GPS logs became part of the investigation.

We discovered it at 11:18 AM. Police painted over it at 7 PM. That's roughly an eight-hour window from discovery to removal. We didn't wait the whole time. We waited 30 minutes initially to see if police responded quickly, then stepped back to let the evidence sit undisturbed. That eight-hour window gave police the time they needed to document, photograph, and collect evidence properly. And because we're on that property regularly, we could narrow the creation window to between Monday afternoon and Wednesday morning—43 hours where it definitely appeared, but not pinpointed to the minute.

Why We Don't Do It Ourselves

Three reasons:

1. Evidence. Hate symbols are crimes. If we remove them, we're destroying evidence. We're interrupting the documentation that police need to investigate, to build a case, to hold someone accountable. Our job isn't to make the symbol disappear; it's to make sure the crime gets recorded.

2. Liability. If we remove a hate symbol ourselves, we're potentially tampering with evidence. We could expose ourselves, the business owner, or the BIA to legal complications. Police have the authority and the training to handle this. We don't.

3. Response. A hate symbol isn't a graffiti problem. It's a safety problem. It's a signal that someone in or passing through the neighborhood felt comfortable leaving a message that says your faith is demonic or your community doesn't belong here. That requires a police response, community accountability, and public acknowledgment that this isn't tolerated. Our job is to notice it and report it. Their job is to respond.

What This Means for Storefronts and BIAs

If you own a business and you see a hate symbol on your storefront or nearby, don't paint over it. Call police. Document it. Let them do their job. Then tell your community what you found and how you responded—not to shame the neighborhood, but to show that you take safety seriously.

If you're on a BIA board and your contractor finds hate speech, your contractor should report it to police, not remove it. That's the professional standard. That's accountability.

If you find hate speech and you're not sure what to do, call police non-emergency. Tell them what you see. They'll guide you from there.

The Symbol We Found, Explained

The Star of David is the primary symbol of Judaism and Jewish identity. Placing "Lucifer" (the devil) beneath it is a direct attempt to link Judaism with Satanism—an echo of the centuries-old "Synagogue of Satan" conspiracy theory that has been used to justify violence against Jewish people. "Ishtar" (an ancient Mesopotamian goddess) is used in occult conspiracy circles to delegitimize the Star of David by claiming it's actually a pagan or demonic symbol. Together, these elements form a coordinated message: Judaism is evil.

Police documented the number inside the star as 188. The sequence contains 88—a white supremacist code for the 8th letter of the alphabet repeated (HH = Heil Hitler, or SS). The surrounding symbols make the intent clear.

This Isn't Our First Time

The July 1 incident on Barton wasn't our first encounter with hate speech. In previous months, we addressed offensive graffiti at Hamilton General Hospital—racial content targeting specific communities. Our approach was the same: prioritize removing the hate content, work transparently with the space's management, and document everything. See our post on that work.

As we wrote at the time: "Our team is dedicated to keeping Hamilton's spaces welcoming and free from hate... we successfully prioritized and cleared the racial content to ensure these discriminatory messages are not displayed in our community."

Why Hamilton Specifically

This isn't paranoia. In 2024, Hamilton Police recorded 297 hate/bias incidents and criminal offences—a 35 per cent increase from 2023. The Black, Jewish, and 2S&LGBTQIA+ communities were most frequently impacted. (Hamilton Police 2024 Hate Crime Statistics)

Historically, according to Statistics Canada data reported by CBC News, Hamilton has had Canada's highest per-capita rate of reported hate crimes—17.1 hate incidents per 100,000 people. The city has seen documented far-right organizing, including protests drawing members of Soldiers of Odin, Wolves of Odin, and Proud Boys. (CBC: "Hamilton Hate Crime")

The landscape is real, documented, and growing.

Three hate incidents in six weeks isn't random. It reflects the documented reality of where we work. And that's exactly why our policy exists: to respond proportionally to an actual problem, not to an imagined one.

This is who we are. It's not one incident. It's a pattern—and it's rooted in something deeper than a business model.

How to Report Hate Symbols in Hamilton

Hamilton Police Non-Emergency: 905-546-4925

What to tell them:

  • The exact location (address or intersection)
  • A description of the symbol(s)
  • When you saw it
  • Your name and contact info

What happens next:

  • Police will assess and document.
  • They may show up quickly or take several hours.
  • They will handle removal or ask the property owner to remove it under police supervision.
  • You can follow up on your report by calling non-emergency with your case number.

If you don't want to call it in yourself: We understand. We know how it could affect you. If you see hate speech but you'd rather not make the call—for any reason—send us a message. We'll report it. No questions, no names needed, no pressure. Reach out at contact@hammerstreetclean.org or text/call 365-357-2405 with the location and what you saw. We'll handle the police report.

For Our Community

If you see hate speech on the streets of Hamilton, don't ignore it. Don't remove it yourself. Report it. Document it. Let the people whose job it is to respond do that job.

And if you're part of a community that's been targeted—if you see symbols designed to threaten you, your faith, your identity—know that when HSC sees it, we're calling the police. We're documenting it. We're treating it as the crime it is.

At the end of the day, we are members of this community first and a business second. While our regular contracts keep the lights on, there are moments where invoices just don't matter. If we encounter targeted, hateful vandalism, we will always step up to handle it 100% for free, straight from the heart. This is what we wrote about that commitment.

True community care means making sure everyone feels safe, valued, and welcome on our streets.
